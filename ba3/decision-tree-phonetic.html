How the Tree is Organised

Four rhythm-and-rhoticity zones. Within each zone, a single phonological feature triggers a variety ID. Every feature has at least one example word with its IPA — if you can't hear or see that contrast, mark the variety ambiguous and fall back to the neighbour-disambiguation table.

Exam-Room Protocol (90 s per ID)

  1. Rhoticity (15 s). Scan for post-vocalic /r/ in car, farm, heart. Rhotic → A, non-rhotic → B, inconsistent → C.
  2. Timing (10 s). Listen for schwa reduction in unstressed syllables. If every syllable is clearly articulated and unreduced, it's syllable-timed.
  3. Zone diagnostic (45 s). Apply the zone's signature feature — SVLR, retroflex stops, /k/-affrication, final-stop deletion, etc.
  4. Commit (20 s). Write the variety name and one clinching IPA example from the clip.
Fatal Trap 14 (SVLR). The rule specifies where vowels are long — before /r v ð z/ and morpheme boundaries. It does not say vowels are short elsewhere by rule. Never write "the rule predicts X is short"; say "X appears short in an environment not listed among the lengthening contexts."
Fatal Trap: rhotic ≠ GAmE. Rhoticity alone does not identify General American. ScotE, IrE, SAE, West Country, IndE, NigE are all rhotic. Always combine rhoticity with a second diagnostic (SVLR · dental stops · diphthong shift · retroflex · voiced fricatives).

Feature Glossary — with IPA Examples

Every feature used as a branch in the walker is defined here with one or two example words. Read this once end-to-end before your first mock run.

Rhoticity
Post-vocalic /r/ pronounced.
rhotic — car [kɑːr] · farm [fɑːrm] · heart [hɑːrt]
non-rhotic — car [kɑː] · farm [fɑːm] · heart [hɑːt]
Stress-timed vs syllable-timed
Stress-timed English reduces unstressed vowels to schwa [ə]; syllable-timed varieties preserve full vowel quality in every syllable.
stress-timed — photograph [ˈfəʊtəɡrɑːf] (three schwas)
syllable-timed — photograph [ˈfotoɡraf] (no schwa; full [o a])
SVLR (Scottish Vowel Length Rule)
Vowels are long before /r v ð z/ or a morpheme boundary; short elsewhere. Diagnostic pairs differ only by the morpheme boundary.
brewed [bruːd] (brew+ed, morpheme boundary → long) vs brood [brʌd] (monomorphemic → short)
greed [ɡriːd] vs agreed [əˈɡriːd] — both long for different reasons
bee [biː] (utterance-final → long) vs beat [bɪt] (short elsewhere)
Dental stops for TH
/θ/ and /ð/ realised as dental [t̪ d̪] (IrE) rather than interdental fricatives.
think [t̪ɪŋk] · this [d̪ɪs] · three [t̪ɹiː]
Contrast: Cockney TH-fronting → think [fɪŋk], this [dɪs] (initial only)
Contrast: AAVE/JC TH-stopping → alveolar [t d], not dental
Kit-raising / SAE diphthong shift
KIT vowel [ɪ] centralises to [ə] in open syllables; PRICE, MOUTH, FACE diphthongs chain-shift.
kit [kət] (vs GAmE [kɪt])
price [prʌɪs] (shifted onset) · mouth [məʊθ] / [mɛʊθ]
West Country voiced initial fricatives
Initial /f s/ voice to [v z]; retroflex or bunched /r/.
farmer [ˈvɑːmə(ɻ)] · Somerset [ˈzʌməɻsɛt] · seven [ˈzɛvən]
T-flapping + Mary-merry-marry merger (GAmE)
Intervocalic /t/ → alveolar flap [ɾ]; /æ ɛ ɛ/ before /r/ all merge.
butter [ˈbʌɾɚ] · latter = ladder [ˈlæɾɚ]
Mary / merry / marry all [ˈmɛri]
Retroflex stops (IndE)
/t d/ realised as retroflex [ʈ ɖ]; stops unaspirated; /v/ and /w/ both [ʋ].
time [ʈaɪm] · dog [ɖɔɡ] · tin [ʈɪn]
pin [pɪn] (no aspiration — contrast RP [pʰɪn])
very [ˈʋɛɾi] · wine = vine [ʋaɪn]
NigE monophthongisation + full unstressed vowels
FACE [e] and GOAT [o] are pure monophthongs; unstressed vowels keep full quality (no schwa).
day [de] (not [deɪ]) · go [ɡo] (not [ɡoʊ])
father [ˈfada] (no schwa in final syllable)
/k/-affrication (Scouse)
Word-final /k/ and sometimes /t/ affricate to [kx] or [ts].
back [bakx] · clock [klɒkx] · book [bʊkx]
Monophthongal FACE / GOAT (Geordie)
FACE and GOAT remain long monophthongs; NURSE is [ɔː].
face [feːs] · goat [ɡoːt] · boat [boːt]
work [wɔːk] (walk = work homophones in broad Geordie)
Cockney cluster: H-drop + L-vocal + TH-front + glottal /t/
Four features nearly always co-occur.
H-dropping — house [aʊs] · hand [ænd]
L-vocalisation — milk [mɪʊk] · tell [teʊ]
TH-fronting — three [friː] · brother [ˈbrʌvə]
Glottal /t/ — butter [ˈbʌʔə] · water [ˈwɔːʔə]
GOOSE-fronting (GAusE, also NZE, young RP)
/uː/ fronts to [ʉː]; HAPPY tenses to [iː]; HRT (high-rising terminal) on statements.
goose [ɡʉːs] · food [fʉːd] · too [tʉː]
city [ˈsɪtiː] · happy [ˈhæpiː]
AAVE cluster: TH-stop + CC-reduction + PIN-PEN
Three co-occurring features; non-rhoticity variable.
TH-stopping — this [dɪs] · three [triː] / [friː]
Cluster reduction — cold [koʊl] · desk [dɛs] · missed [mɪs]
PIN-PEN merger — pin = pen [pɪn] · ten = tin
RP — conservative reference
Non-rhotic, stress-timed, TRAP-BATH split, no H-drop, no TH-front, unfronted GOOSE.
bath [bɑːθ] vs trap [træp]
class [klɑːs] · father [ˈfɑːðə]
goose [ɡuːs] (back, not fronted) · three [θriː] (interdental, not fronted)
SinE final-stop deletion / glottal
Word-final stops deleted or glottalised; no aspiration; monophthongal FACE/GOAT; final-particle-style intonation.
cat [kæʔ] or [kæ] · stop [stɒʔ] · pick [pɪʔ]
face [feːs] · go [ɡoː]
JC (Jamaican Creole) phonological cluster
TH-stopping universal; H dropped (or hypercorrected); consonant clusters simplified; characteristic PRICE-MOUTH diphthongs.
this [dɪs] · three [triː] · thing [tɪŋ]
hand [an] · house [aʊs] / [aus]
time [taɪm] · town [tɔŋ]
Gullah phonological cluster
TH-stopping, cluster simplification (as JC), plus residual tonal patterning and more archaic/conservative vowels rooted in 18c Plantation English + West African substrate.
this [dɪs] · three [tri] · brother [ˈbrʌda]
Retention of archaic [ʊ] in some lexical sets; tonal accent on stressed syllables
HCE (Hawaiʻi Creole) phonological cluster
Reduced vowel inventory (5–7 vowels), glottalisation of final stops, rising-falling intonation contours from Pacific substrate.
like [laɪʔ] · what [wɐʔ] · eat [iːʔ]
Tendency toward [d] for /ð/ and [t] for /θ/, as in JC/AAVE
