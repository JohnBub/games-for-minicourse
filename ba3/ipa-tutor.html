What IPA is, and what it is not

The International Phonetic Alphabet is a one-to-one mapping between symbols and speech sounds. It exists because English spelling isn't — the letter c in cat, cell, and ocean represents three different sounds. IPA fixes the symbol, not the spelling, so that [k] always means one sound, no matter what language it appears in.

When you read a transcription, the brackets tell you what kind of claim is being made. Slashes / / mark a phonemic transcription — a claim about which meaningful sound-categories a word contains. Square brackets [ ] mark a phonetic transcription — a claim about how the word was actually produced, including fine detail. In practice, a line like ['fɑːðə] uses square brackets because it records what a specific speaker did, down to the length mark and the final schwa.

Read me aloud. The fastest way to learn IPA is to pronounce the symbol as you read it, checking yourself against an example word. This tutor lets you click any symbol to hear an English word it appears in. Use it.

How to approach this tutor

Work through the tabs in order the first time. Consonants is the gentlest start: each symbol appears in a chart sorted by where and how the sound is made in your mouth. Vowels is the hardest because vowels have no contact point — you learn them by their position in an imaginary mouth-shaped quadrilateral. Lexical Sets are Wells's way of tying vowels to keywords: when you see kit you know you mean the vowel in bid, regardless of variety.

Diacritics are the small marks that sit on or around a symbol to record fine detail: length, aspiration, dark-l, nasalisation. They are the bread and butter of variety description. Varieties lines up ScotE, SAE, and RP so you can see which features differ. Practice is a click-through quiz.

A worked example: your father / harder clip

You saw the transcription ['fɑːðə] for father and ['hɑːrdɚ] for harder. Read the first one left to right:

  • [ˈ] is a stress mark — the syllable to the right is stressed.
  • [f] is a voiceless labiodental fricative (top teeth on bottom lip).
  • [ɑː] is an open back unrounded vowel, held long (the colon-like mark [ː] means length).
  • [ð] is the voiced th of this.
  • [ə] is schwa — an unstressed central vowel.

Now harder: everything works the same, but the [r] after the long [ɑː] is pronounced (the speaker is rhotic in that position), and the final syllable is written [dɚ] — an r-coloured schwa, meaning the schwa and the r are produced together as a single rhotic vowel. Variable rhoticity inside a single clip is exactly the kind of fine detail IPA was built to record.

Cells show place (where), manner (how), and voicing.

How to read this chart

Columns are place of articulation — where in the mouth the constriction happens, moving from the front (bilabial = both lips) to the back (glottal = vocal folds). Rows are manner of articulation — how the airstream is obstructed. A plosive blocks the air completely then releases it; a fricative squeezes it through a narrow gap; a nasal redirects it through the nose; an approximant barely obstructs it at all.

Voicing is the third dimension: your vocal folds either vibrate (voiced, like [b z v ð]) or stay apart (voiceless, like [p s f θ]). Touch your throat and say sssss then zzzzz — the buzz in the second one is voicing.

Vowels vary on three dimensions: height (close–open), backness (front–back), and rounding.

The three dimensions of a vowel

Height is how far the tongue is from the roof of the mouth. In [i] (FLEECE) the tongue is high and close; in [a] the jaw is dropped and the tongue is low. Backness is where the tongue's highest point sits — [i] is front, [u] is back. Rounding describes the lips: [u] rounds, [i] spreads. English mostly rounds its back vowels, which is why RP [ɔː] (THOUGHT) feels different from the unrounded [ɑː] (PALM).

Vowels can also be long (written with the length mark [ː]) or short, tense or lax, and they can glide from one position to another — a diphthong like [aɪ] in PRICE is a single vowel that moves as you say it.

Wells's Lexical Sets

Because the same vowel can have different IPA values across varieties (the PALM vowel is [ɑː] in RP but can be [a] or [ɑ] in GAusE), John Wells (1982) proposed to name each vowel after a keyword: the vowel in bid is simply the KIT vowel, everywhere. This is the common currency your textbooks use.

Why lexical sets matter for you

When Stuart-Smith writes that Scottish English has no length contrast in the BATH set, she isn't talking about the spelling — she's talking about every word that patterns like bath, grass, pass, laugh, staff, dance, sample, plant. RP lengthens these to [ɑː]; most ScotE speakers keep them short [a], identical to the TRAP vowel. The keyword saves you paragraphs of wordlists.

Three lexical-set splits are constantly invoked in our course: TRAP/BATH (where a variety splits trap from bath), LOT/THOUGHT (the cot–caught merger in much of GA), and FOOT/STRUT (merged in northern English, split in RP). Learn these three splits cold.

Diacritics

Diacritics are the small marks that modify a base symbol. They are how IPA records fine articulatory detail — and they are where narrow phonetic transcription earns its keep in variety studies.

The diacritics you will actually use in this course

Four diacritics do most of the work in our lectures. Length [ː] is everywhere: the Scottish Vowel Length Rule (SVLR), for instance, is a claim about when the length mark is justified. Aspiration [ʰ] distinguishes most English syllable-initial voiceless stops — pen is [pʰɛn], not [pɛn]. Velarisation [ˠ] or [ɫ] marks the “dark” l that Bauer (28) and Stuart-Smith (63) write about for ScotE. Nasalisation [̃] — the tilde above a vowel — records the vowel-through-nose resonance you hear in French bon or in anticipatory-nasalisation varieties like Nigerian English.

Reading a feature line in practice

Take rhoticity. In RP, harder is [ˈhɑːdə] — the orthographic r is silent, leaving a long vowel and a final schwa. In SAE, the same word is [ˈhɑːɹdɚ] — the r is produced both pre-consonantally and as an r-coloured schwa. In ScotE, rhoticity is variable and often realised with a tap [ɾ] rather than an approximant [ɹ]: [ˈhɑɾdəɾ]. The clip you were analysing — father non-rhotic, harder rhotic — is exactly what Stuart-Smith (57) means when she calls ScotE rhoticity “variable.”

Now take the SVLR: in ScotE, the vowel is long only before a voiced fricative, r, or a morpheme boundary. So the FLEECE vowel in greed is short [i], but in agreed it is long [iː] — same spelling, different morphological structure. Diacritics alone tell you which claim is being made.
