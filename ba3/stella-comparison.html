GMU Stella Passage — Variety Comparison

The eleven varieties below each realise the same elicitation passage ("Please call Stella. Ask her to bring these things with her from the store . . .") spoken by one native or representative speaker per variety, drawn from the GMU Speech Accent Archive. For each variety the five most diagnostic phonological features are highlighted inline in the verified IPA transcription and unpacked in the cards that follow, modelled on the midterm PEA structure (point–evidence–analysis) with explicit predictable/unpredictable judgements and hypotheses where the speaker departs from the course description.

Colour legend: consonant vowel prosody / suprasegmental structural / other. IPA transcriptions preserve every diacritic from the verified GMU-Transcriptions-FINAL.md (two-pass arbiter methodology, 2026-04-22). Page references marked (?) are inherited from the feature-analysis files and must be verified against the source PDF before use in graded work. Three samples (NigE, IndE, SgE) use L2 proxy speakers — see the caveat box within each section.

1. RP — English13 (London-area, female)

[pʰiiz kʰɒlˠ stelə ask ɚ tʰu bɹĩŋ ðiz θĩŋz wɪθ hɹ fɹʌ̃m ðə stoə sɪks spũːnz ʌv fɹɛʃ snou pʰiːz faɹy θɪk slæbz ʌv bluː tʃiːz æn meɪbi ə snæk fə hə bɹʌðə bɒb wi ɒlso nid ə smɒl pʰlæstɪk sneɪk æn̩ ə bɪg tʰɔɪ fɹɒg fə ðə kɪdz ʃi kʰæ̃n skup ðiz θĩŋz ɪntʰu θɹi ɹɛd bægz æn wi wɪl go mit hə ɑ̃n wẽnzdeɪ æt˺ ðə tɹẽɪn stɛɪʃən]
1 Non-rhoticity Predictable
Evidence store [stoə], her [hə], brother [bɹʌðə], for [fə]. Post-vocalic /r/ is systematically absent; NORTH surfaces as centring diphthong [oə].
Rule "Varieties of English in both Scotland and Ireland are rhotic . . . although the quality of the /r/ is different in the two cases" (Bauer 28). The corresponding rule for RP is that "post-vocalic /r/ is lost" (Ihsane, "Lecture 3" § 1.2, 2026).
2 TRAP–BATH split (London-fronted BATH) Partly predictable
Evidence ask [ask] (short front [a]) vs. TRAP plastic [pʰlæstɪk]. BATH remains distinct from TRAP but is not the canonical long back [ɑː].
Rule "Southern / RP: trap [træp] ≠ bath [bɑːθ] (long /ɑː/)" (Ihsane, "Lecture 3" § 1.3, 2026).
Hypothesis The fronted/shortened [a] reflects contemporary London-RP shortening of BATH — sociolectally consistent with the speaker bio rather than a textbook-conservative RP.
3 Diphthongal GOAT and FACE Predictable
Evidence snow [snou], maybe [meɪbi], snake [sneɪk], train [tɹẽɪn].
Rule "FACE / GOAT — RP [eɪ], [əʊ] diphthongs; ScotE [e], [o] monophthongs" (Ihsane, "Lecture 4" § 3.11, 2026).
4 Dental fricatives retained Predictable
Evidence these [ðiz], things [θĩŋz], with [wɪθ], three [θɹi] — no TH-stopping, no TH-fronting.
Rule TH is retained as dental fricative in conservative RP, in contrast to "Southern Hiberno-English [which] frequently replaces the dental fricatives . . . with dental plosives" (Bauer 28).
5 Clear/dark [l] allophony Predictable
Evidence Clear in onset — Stella [stelə], plastic [pʰlæstɪk], also [ɒlso]; dark in coda — call [kʰɒlˠ]. Will [wɪl] is transcribed without the velarisation diacritic but is phonologically expected to be dark by the same rule.
Rule "In RP, the distribution is allophonic: clear [l] occurs in the onset (before the vowel) and dark [lˠ] in the coda (after the vowel)" (Haeberli 105, 115).

2. ScotE — English24 (Edinburgh / Glasgow-area, female)

[pʰliz kɔlˠ stelə ask ɚ tə bɾɪŋ ðiːz θĩŋz wɪθ hɚ fɹʌ̃m ðə stou sɪks spɒ̃ŋz ʌv fɹɛʃ sno: pʰiːz faɪv θɪkʲ slæːbz ʌ bluː tʃiːz ɛ̃n meɪbi ə snækʰ fɚ ə bɪʌðɚ bɔbʰ wi ɒlsə nid ʌ smɒːlˠ pʰlˠæːstɪk sneɪk ɛn ə bɪkʰ tʰɒɪ fɾɔɡ fɚ ðə kɪts ʃi gɛ̃n skʊp ðis sĩŋz ɪntə θɾiː ɹɛd bæɡɡz ə̃n wɪlˠ gɔ mit hɚ ɑ̃n weʃnzdei ɛtʰ ðə tɾeɪn steɪʃə̃n]
1 Dark /l/ in all positions Predictable
Evidence Coda — call [kɔlˠ], small [smɒːlˠ], will [wɪlˠ]. Onset — plastic [pʰlˠæːstɪk]. Plain [l] occasionally surfaces (Stella, please), consistent with Stuart-Smith's "tendency" caveat.
Rule "/l/ is dark in all positions in Scottish English and clear in all positions in Irish varieties" (Bauer 28); "the secondary articulation of /l/ tends to be dark in all positions in the word" (Stuart-Smith 63).
2 Rhoticity with tapped /r/ Predictable
Evidence bring [bɾɪŋ], three [θɾiː], frog [fɾɔɡ], train [tɾeɪn]; post-vocalic her [hɚ], brother [bɪʌðɚ].
Rule "Scots is usually said to favour taps, though Johnston (1997: 510) notes that [ɹ], more typical of Scottish Standard English, is encroaching" (Stuart-Smith 63).
3 SVLR — length conditioned by environment Predictable
Evidence Long in SVLR contexts — three [θɾiː] (word-boundary), cheese [tʃiːz], these [ðiːz] (before voiced fricative). Short elsewhere — scoop [skʊp].
Rule "Scottish Vowel Length Rule . . . vowels are phonetically long in certain environments: before voiced fricatives, before /r/, and before a boundary, including a morpheme boundary" (Stuart-Smith 56–57); "the monophthongs /i, u/ and the diphthong /ai/ alone are subject to the SVLR" (Stuart-Smith 57).
4 TH-substitution ([s] for /θ/) Partly unpredictable
Evidence things [θĩŋz] (1st) retains [θ] but [sĩŋz] (2nd) shows substitution; three [θɾiː] keeps [θ] — fronting is variable.
Rule "the /θ/ in 'thick' is pronounced as a /f/, and the /ð/ as in 'rather' is pronounced as a /v/" (Ihsane, "Lecture 4" § 3.10, 2026).
Hypothesis [s] before [ĩŋ] may reflect assimilatory coarticulation — the following nasal-velar sequence disfavours labiodental [f], so the tongue-tip shifts to [s]. Alternatively, a speaker-specific Urban-Scots /θ/→[s] variant.
5 Monophthongal GOAT (diphthongal FACE) Partly predictable
Evidence GOAT — snow [sno:], go [gɔ]. FACE — maybe [meɪbi], snake [sneɪk] (diphthongal).
Rule "Monophthongal FACE [e] and GOAT [o] — where RP has diphthongs [eɪ], [əʊ] . . . ScotE holds a single steady vowel" (Ihsane, "Lecture 4" § 0, 2026).
Hypothesis The split (GOAT monophthong, FACE diphthong) places this speaker toward the ScStE-anglicised end of Stuart-Smith's (53–54) continuum — realistic for an Edinburgh-area read-speech elicitation.

3. Northern Irish English — English14 (Belfast / Ulster-area)

[pʰliːz kʰɑl stɛlə æsk ɚ ɹə bɾĩŋ ðiz θĩŋz wɪθ hɚ fɹʌm ðə stoɻ sɪks spũŋz ə fɹɛːʃ snö pʰɹiːz faɾy θɪk slæbz əv blu tʃiːz ɛn mebi ə snæk fə ɚ bɹʌðɚ bɑɻbʰ wi alsɒ nid ə smal pʰlæstɪk sneɪk ɛn ə bɪg tʰɔɪ fɹoɪɡ fə ðə kɪdz ʃi kɛ̃n skup ðiːz θĩŋz ĩnɹə θɹi ɹɛd bæɪgz æ̃n wi wɪl gö ɹə mit hɚ wẽːnzdɛɪ æt ðə tɾẽn stɛʃɪn]
1 Rhoticity with retroflex quality Predictable
Evidence store [stoɻ], Bob [bɑɻbʰ], her [hɚ], brother [bɹʌðɚ]. Retroflex [ɻ] surfaces especially in coda.
Rule "Varieties of English in both Scotland and Ireland are rhotic . . . although the quality of the /r/ is different" (Bauer 28); Hickey identifies retroflex [ɻ] as characteristic of the Ulster/north–south transitional area (Hickey 74 (?), in Kortmann and Schneider).
2 Dental fricatives retained (unlike Southern IrE) Predictable
Evidence these [ðiz], things [θĩŋz], with [wɪθ], three [θɹi], brother [bɹʌðɚ] — fricative throughout; no stopping.
Rule "In the entire area of Ulster the THIN and THIS lexical sets show fricatives" (Hickey 78–79 (?), in Kortmann and Schneider), contrasting with Southern Hiberno-English stopping (Bauer 28).
3 Clear /l/ throughout (non-Belfast) Partly unpredictable
Evidence call [kʰɑl], Stella [stɛlə], small [smal], will [wɪl] — no velarisation diacritic in any position.
Rule "/l/ is dark in all positions in Scottish English and clear in all positions in Irish varieties" (Bauer 28). Ihsane qualifies: "Belfast (Ulster): [ɫ] in all positions (dark). Probably a contact feature via the Ulster Plantation" (Ihsane, "Lecture 4" § 3.9, 2026).
Hypothesis The speaker aligns with the Irish (clear-/l/) pole rather than the Belfast (dark-/l/) pole, suggesting origin outside urban Belfast (e.g. western Ulster or Donegal) or register-driven suppression of a stigmatised dark realisation in read speech.
4 TRAP-raising to [ɛ] pre-nasally Partly predictable
Evidence can [kɛ̃n] — [ɛ] where RP has [æ]. Compare plastic [pʰlæstɪk], snack [snæk] — [æ] elsewhere; raising is pre-nasal.
Rule Hickey notes Ulster raising/centring of short vowels, with TRAP raising to [ɛ] in certain environments (Hickey 78 (?)).
Hypothesis The pre-nasal raising reflects the cross-dialectally common /æ/-raising-before-nasal pattern interacting with Ulster's general raising tendency; an Ulster-Scots contact influence via Urban-Scots KIT/DRESS raising (Stuart-Smith 57) is also plausible.
5 Central-onset GOAT [ö] Predictable
Evidence snow [snö], go [gö]; FACE more variable (maybe [mebi], snake [sneɪk]).
Rule "When mid front vowels occur in stressed position, they tend to develop off-glides" (Hickey 80 (?)); central-onset GOAT is a recognised Ulster pattern (Hickey 78 (?)).

4. Irish English — English72 (Republic of Ireland, Dublin-area likely)

[pʰliːz kʰəl stelə ask ɚ ɾə bɾũŋ ðis θĩŋz wɪθ ɚ fɹʌm ðə stoɻ sɪks spũnz əy fɹɛʃ sno pʰiːz faɪv t̪ʰɪk slabz ə blu tʃʰiz ʔɛn meɪbi ə snæk fə ə bɹʌðɚ bab wi ʌlso niɻ ə smɑːl pʰlæsɪk sneɪk ɛn ə bɪg tʰɔɪ fɾɒɡ fə ðə kʰɪdz ʃi kən skup ðɪz θĩŋz ɪntu θɹi ɹɛd bæɡs ən wi wɪl go mɪɾ ɚ wɛ̃nzeɪ æt˺ ðəʔ tʰɹeɪn steɪʃə̃n]
1 Dental stops [t̪, d̪] for /θ, ð/ (TH-stopping) Predictable
Evidence thick [t̪ʰɪk] — dental diacritic visible. Variable: these [ðis], things [θĩŋz], three [θɹi] preserve the fricative.
Rule "Southern Hiberno-English frequently replaces the dental fricatives in words like thin and that with dental plosives" (Bauer 28); "/θ/ and /ð/ realised as dental stops [t̪, d̪] in all positions in Southern IrE" (Ihsane, "Lecture 4" § 3.11, 2026).
2 Clear /l/ in all positions Predictable
Evidence Onset — please [pʰliːz], Stella [stelə], blue [blu]. Coda — call [kʰəl], small [smɑːl], will [wɪl]. No velarisation diacritics.
Rule "/l/ is dark in all positions in Scottish English and clear in all positions in Irish varieties" (Bauer 28).
3 Rhoticity with retroflex tendency Predictable
Evidence store [stoɻ], need [niɻ] (retroflex intervocalically), her [ɚ], brother [bɹʌðɚ]; weak-form for [fə].
Rule "Varieties of English in both Scotland and Ireland are rhotic . . . although the quality of the /r/ is different" (Bauer 28); Southern IrE /r/ described as "retroflex [ɻ] (south)" (Ihsane, "Lecture 4" § 3.9, 2026).
4 /t/-lenition / deletion Partly unpredictable
Evidence plastic [pʰlæsɪk] — medial /t/ deleted; meet [mɪɾ] — intervocalic tap; Wednesday [wɛ̃nzeɪ] — medial /d/ deleted; the [ðəʔ] — glottal.
Rule Southern IrE realises /t, d/ as "apical slit fricatives" with frication, "not generally to be found in the north" (Hickey 79 (?)); "/t/ and /d/ realised as apical slit fricatives . . . Southern IrE only; not in Ulster" (Ihsane, "Lecture 4" § 3.12, 2026).
Hypothesis [pʰlæsɪk] may represent the endpoint of the IrE lenition cline (stop → slit fricative → approximant → deletion); in casual reading the fricative release weakens to zero in high-frequency items. Possibly also a Dublin-area innovation, since Dublin is a noted locus of phonological change.
5 Diphthongal FACE [eɪ] Predictable
Evidence maybe [meɪbi], snake [sneɪk], train [tʰɹeɪn], station [steɪʃə̃n], Wednesday [wɛ̃nzeɪ].
Rule Course baseline: "ScotE [e]" monophthongal FACE vs. RP's diphthongal [eɪ] (Ihsane, "Lecture 4" § 3.11, 2026); IrE patterns with RP.

5. General American — English1 (Midwestern / GA reference)

[pʰliiːz kʰɑlˠ stelə æsk ɚ ɾə bɾĩŋ ðiːz θĩŋz wɪθ ɚ fɹʌm ðə stɔɹ sɪks spũːnz ʌv fɹɛʃ snou pʰiːz faɪ:v θɪk slæːbz əv bluː tʃiːz æn meɪbi ə snækʰ fɚ hɚ bɹʌðɚ bɑːb wiː ɑːlˠso nɪɾɚ ə smalˠ pʰlæstɪkʰ sneɪk æ̃n ə bɪɡ tʰɔɪ fɹɑːɡ fɚ ðə kʰɪdz ʃiː kɑn skʷuːpʰ ðiːz θĩŋz ɪntə θɹi ɹɛd bæːɡz æːn wiː wɪlˠ goʊ mitʰ hə wɛnzdei æt˺ ðə tʰɹeɪn steɪʃn̩]
1 Rhoticity Predictable
Evidence her [ɚ], store [stɔɹ], brother [bɹʌðɚ], for [fɚ], frog [fɹɑːɡ].
Rule "GA, Canadian, Scottish and Irish varieties of English are rhotic . . . RP, Australian, New Zealand and South African Englishes are non-rhotic" (Bauer 78). Puskás's matrix lists SAE as "mainly rhotic" (Puskás, Chapter 4 4).
2 Intervocalic /t/-flapping Predictable
Evidence to [ɾə] (after ask her); need a [nɪɾɚ].
Rule "In some varieties a tap is heard in such environments instead, giving [bʌɾə], and this may be considered standard, for example in Canada, the USA, Australia and New Zealand" (Bauer 109); SAE t = "intervocalic t as flap: ɾ" (Puskás, Chapter 4 4).
3 LOT unrounded to [ɑ(ː)] Predictable
Evidence Bob [bɑːb], frog [fɹɑːɡ], also [ɑːlˠso]. Compare RP [bɒb, fɹɒg, ɒlso].
Rule "Some varieties of North American English have the same vowel in the THOUGHT lexical set as in the LOT lexical set" (Bauer 80); Puskás gives SAE LOT as "α ~ σ" vs. RP [ɒ] (Puskás, Chapter 4 3).
4 Velarised /l/ generalised Predictable
Evidence call [kʰɑlˠ], small [smalˠ], also [ɑːlˠso], will [wɪlˠ]. Dark [lˠ] extends to pre-vocalic-at-word-boundary.
Rule "RP has a more palatalised version of /l/ before a vowel, while most other standard international varieties have a rather darker version of /l/ in this position" (Bauer 77).
5 GOAT as back-rounded [oʊ] Partly predictable
Evidence snow [snou], go [goʊ], also [ɑːlˠso]. The RP sample in this pair also shows [snou], so the diagnostic rests on the broader pattern.
Rule Puskás, Chapter 4: GOAT = "σo" (RP /əʊ/) vs. "o ~ σσ" in SAE (Puskás 3); W5a describes SAE GOAT as [oʊ] with back-rounded onset (Ihsane 2026, W5a § 1.3).
Hypothesis The GA [goʊ] remains diagnostic only when paired with rhoticity, flapping, LOT-unrounding; the RP speaker's convergent [snou] may reflect younger-RP narrowing of GOAT toward a back-rounded variant.

6. General Australian English — English3

[pʰliːz kʰɒlˠ stelə ask hɚ tʰu bɹĩŋ ðis ʃĩŋz wɪθ hə fɹʌm ðə stoə sɪks spũːŋz ə fɹɛʃ snəʊ pʰiːz faɹy θɪk slæbz əv bluː tʃiːz æn meɪbi ə snæk fə hə bɹʌðə bɒb wi ɒlso nid ə smɒl pʰlæstɪk sneɪk æn ə bɪɡ tʰɔɪ fɹɒɡ fə ðə kʰɪdz ʃi kʰẽn skuːp ðiːz ʃĩŋz ɪntʰu θɾi ɾɛd bæɡz æn wi wɪl gəʊ mit hə wẽnzdeɪ æʔ ðə tɹeɪn steʃɪn]
1 Non-rhoticity with linking /r/ Predictable
Evidence her [hə] (pre-consonantal) vs. ask her to [hɚ] (pre-vocalic linking r); brother [bɹʌðə], for [fə], store [stoə].
Rule "RP, Australian, New Zealand and South African Englishes are non-rhotic" (Bauer 78); GAusE r = "Non-rhotic" (Puskás, Chapter 4 4).
2 Diphthongised GOAT with central onset Predictable
Evidence go [gəʊ], snow [snəʊ]. Compare RP [snou, go] and GA [goʊ].
Rule Puskás lists GAusE GOAT as "σσ" (i.e. [əʊ]) vs. RP's "σo" (Puskás, Chapter 4 3); W5b: "GOAT: /əʊ/ → /əʉ/ (central-fronted offset)" (Ihsane 2026, W5b § 1.3).
3 GOOSE relatively unfronted Partly unpredictable
Evidence scoop [skuːp], blue [bluː], spoons [spũːŋz], into [ɪntʰu] — [u(ː)] close-back, not fronted [ʉː].
Rule "Fronted GOOSE [ʉː]" is listed as a GAusE diagnostic (Ihsane 2026, W5b § 0); GAusE GOOSE = "uσ/σu" (Puskás, Chapter 4 3).
Hypothesis The speaker may sit at the Cultivated end of the Broad–General–Cultivated continuum (roughly 10% of the population per Puskás, Chapter 4 2), where GOOSE fronting has not completed; alternatively, the GMU rendering may under-specify the backness diacritic.
4 DRESS raising toward [e] (uneven) Partly unpredictable
Evidence Raised in Stella [stelə], station [steʃɪn]; not raised in fresh [fɹɛʃ], red [ɾɛd].
Rule GAusE DRESS listed as "e" vs. RP's "e ~ ε" and SAE's "ε" (Puskás, Chapter 4 3).
Hypothesis DRESS-raising is lexically uneven — CVCV words (Stella, station) accept the raising, whereas closed-syllable CVC words (fresh, red) resist it. Phonotactically, DRESS before a voiced stop or sibilant may resist the push upward. This mirrors the "DRESS reversal" anomaly noted in the midterm corrigé (Puskás, Midterm Corrigé (?)).
5 Word-final T-glottalling Unpredictable
Evidence at [æʔ]. RP sample has [æt˺] (unreleased stop).
Rule "Extensive use of a glottal stop either intervocalically or word finally . . . is characterised as 'a non-standard accent in most varieties of English'" (Bauer 109); "a certain amount of glottal use is compatible with standard status, as long as it is not intervocalic" (Bauer 109).
Hypothesis Word-final glottalling suggests contact with non-standard urban Australian or Cockney-style T-glottalling — one of four "pronunciation features [shared] with Cockney" in colonial Southern-Hemisphere varieties (Bauer 109).

7. New Zealand English — English41

[a: pliːz kol stelə æsk ə ɾə bɾĩŋ ðis θĩŋz wɪθ hə fɹʌ̃m nə stoə sɪks spəʊnz əy fɹɛʃ snɘʊ pɪːz im meɪbi ə snæk fɚ hə bɹʌðə bɒbʰ wi ɒlsoʊ niːd ə smɒl plæstɪk snaɪk æ̃n ə bɪɡ tɒɪ fɾɒɡ fə ðə kɪdz ʃi kɛ̃n skuːpʰ ðiz θĩŋz ɪ̃ntü θɹi ɹɛd bæɡz ə̃n wi wɪl gäʊ miːt hə wɪnzdeɪ æt ðə tɹeɪn stɛɪʃə̃n]
1 Centralised KIT vowel Partly predictable
Evidence Wednesday [wɪnzdeɪ], kids [kɪdz], with [wɪθ], into [ɪ̃ntü].
Rule "The KIT vowel is a well-known shibboleth for distinguishing Australians from New Zealanders. Australians accuse New Zealanders of saying fush and chups for fish and chips" (Bauer 77).
Hypothesis A younger-urban NZE speaker (Auckland, Christchurch) with less extreme centralisation than older Southland/rural varieties; the careful GMU reading-style also inhibits the full shibboleth realisation.
2 Non-rhoticity Predictable
Evidence her [hə], brother [bɹʌðə], for [fə], store [stoə].
Rule "RP, Australian, New Zealand and South African Englishes are non-rhotic, as is the English in parts of the Atlantic States in the United States" (Bauer 78); exception: "the English in a small area in the south of New Zealand" is rhotic (Bauer 78).
3 Chain-shifted FACE with lowered onset Partly unpredictable
Evidence snake [snaɪk] (lowered onset, overlapping PRICE); maybe [meɪbi], Wednesday [wɪnzdeɪ] retain near-RP [eɪ].
Rule Bauer's Figure 6.2 places NZE FACE and DRESS pulled upward, with FACE's onset lowered toward the area vacated by PRICE (Bauer 77 (?) — figure referenced, not verbatim quoted).
Hypothesis The NZE push-chain is lexically diffused: frequent words (maybe, Wednesday) are more conservative; lower-frequency targets (snake) realise the shift more completely, consistent with the lexical-diffusion model of chain-shift spread.
4 TRAP→DRESS raising (short front vowel shift) Predictable
Evidence can [kɛ̃n] — TRAP raised to DRESS area; Stella [stelə] — DRESS raised to [e]; fresh [fɹɛʃ], red [ɹɛd] preserve [ɛ].
Rule "For many speakers of New Zealand English if there is a following /l/ . . . Alan and Ellen are homophones" — pointer to the TRAP/DRESS push-chain (Bauer 79).
5 NEAR–SQUARE merger Not testable
Evidence The passage elicits no NEAR/SQUARE minimal pairs. store [stoə] is the only centring diphthong.
Rule "beer and bear: often not distinguished in New Zealand English" (Bauer 80, Figure 6.4).
Hypothesis The feature should be retained as a GA-vs-NZE near-neighbour diagnostic for the exam, using a supplementary elicitation containing beer, bear, hair, here (Ihsane 2026, Derivative-A § A.3 (?)).

8. Jamaican English — English4

[pʰiːz kəl stelʌ ask hɛɹ tʰu biŋ ðiːz θĩŋz wɪθ hɛɹ fɹʌm ðə stoɹ sɪks spuːnz ʌv fɹɛːʃ sno pʰiːz faɪv θɪkʰ slæbz ʌf blu tʃiːz æ̃nd meɪbi ə snæk fɔɹ hɛɹ bɹʌðə bab wi ɒlso nid ə smɒl pʰlæstɪk sneɪk æ̃nd ə bɪɡ tʰɔɪ fɾɒɡ fɔɹ ðə kɪdz ʃi kʰẽn skuːpʰ ðiz θĩŋz ɪntʰu θɹiː ɹɛd bæɡz æn wi wɪl goʊ nekʰ go mit hɛɹ wẽnzdeɪ æt˺ ðə tɹẽɪn steɪʃə̃n]
1 Dental fricatives preserved Predictable
Evidence things [θĩŋz], three [θɹiː], thick [θɪkʰ], these [ðiːz], the [ðə] — fricative throughout.
Rule "the JamE linked variant, [θ], . . . occurring in 88% of the occurrences of this variable" (Devonish and Harry 475); JamE, unlike JamC, possesses /θ, ð/ in its consonant inventory (Devonish and Harry 473).
2 Variable rhoticity Predictable
Evidence Rhotic in store [stoɹ], for [fɔɹ], her [hɛɹ], three [θɹiː]; non-rhotic in brother [bɹʌðə].
Rule "JamE is generally rhotic" (Devonish and Harry 476).
Hypothesis Unstressed final-syllable /r/ (brother) is the environment where rhoticity is least robust cross-linguistically; one non-rhotic token is consistent with the "general but not categorical" framing.
3 FACE / GOAT mixed monophthong-diphthong Predictable
Evidence Monophthong — snow [sno], go [go]. Diphthong — maybe [meɪbi], snake [sneɪk], go [goʊ].
Rule "the idealised JamE variants are [oː] and [eː]" for GOAT and FACE; JamC diphthongs "are not part of the idealised phonological system of JamE" (Devonish and Harry 463).
Hypothesis The mix instantiates Devonish and Harry's "differential convergence" (475): the speaker alternates across tokens rather than selecting the creole pole or the metropolitan pole categorically.
4 Onset /pl-/ and /br-/ cluster simplification Unpredictable
Evidence please [pʰiːz] (no /l/); bring [biŋ] (no /r/). Compare RP [pʰiiz, bɹĩŋ].
Rule Devonish and Harry describe cluster simplification primarily at syllable codas, not in word-initial onsets, and list initial /pl, br/ within JamE's inventory (Devonish and Harry 473).
Hypothesis JamC-style onset reduction surfacing in JamE output; alternatively a lexicalised pronunciation of the highly frequent items please and bring. Either way, it does not reflect the "idealised JamE" system.
5 Unstressed non-rhotic brother as convergence marker Predictable
Evidence brother [bɹʌðə] — the sole non-rhotic token in an otherwise rhotic idiolect.
Rule JamE rhoticity is "general" but not categorical (Devonish and Harry 476); differential convergence predicts selective token-level alignment with metropolitan non-rhotic varieties (Devonish and Harry 475).

9. Nigerian English — Yoruba2 (L1 Yoruba — L2 proxy)

Speaker-substitution caveat. No native-English L1 speaker from Nigeria in the GMU archive displayed the institutionalised NigE phonology described in Gut's chapter with a full transcription. The L1 Yoruba speaker is used as a proxy because L2 Yoruba-accented features overlap substantially with the NigE sub-variety Gut labels "Yoruba English" (Gut 820–23). Read as an approximation; L2-specific interference is flagged in the hypotheses.
[pʰlis kɒl stelə æsk hæː tŭ bɾĩŋ ðis θĩŋz wɪθ hæ fɹʌm ðə stɔː sɪks spũnz əy fɹɛʃ sno pɪːz faɪv tɪk slæːbz əv blu tʃiːz æn meɪbi e snæk fɔ hæ bɹʌðə bɒb wi also nid e smɒl pʰlæstɪk sneɪk æn e bɪɡ tʰɔɪ fɹɔɡ fə ðə kʰiːdz ʃi kæn skup ðis ðĩŋz ɪntu θɹi ɹɛd bæɡz æn wi wi gou mit hæ wẽnzdeɪ at ðə tʃɹẽɪn steɪʃən]
1 Variable TH-stopping Predictable
Evidence Stopped — thick [tɪk]; preserved — things [θĩŋz], three [θɹi], with [wɪθ]; voiced-TH alternation — things [ðĩŋz] (2nd occurrence).
Rule Gut lists Yoruba English realisations of /θ/ as "[θ], [t], [ṭ]" and of /ð/ as "[ð], [d], [ḍ]" (Gut 823).
Hypothesis Lexical diffusion — high-frequency function words (the, this) pattern differently from lower-frequency content words (thick), consistent with the variable distribution Gut documents.
2 Non-rhoticity Predictable
Evidence her [hæ], store [stɔː], brother [bɹʌðə], for [fɔ]. Every post-vocalic /r/ is absent.
Rule "NigE is non-rhotic" (Gut 822).
3 FLEECE–KIT quality merger / length neutralisation Predictable
Evidence please [pʰlis] (short FLEECE), peas [pɪːz] (KIT-quality with length), call [kɒl].
Rule In Yoruba English and Igbo English, the FLEECE vowel "is shorter and the same as in KIT" (Gut 820).
4 Syllable-timed rhythm / no vowel reduction Predictable
Evidence maybe [meɪbi] (full [i]), plastic [pʰlæstɪk] (no schwa), Wednesday [wẽnzdeɪ] (full [eɪ]), a [e] rather than [ə].
Rule "Vowel reduction does not occur in NigE, which results in a more equal duration of syllables" (Gut 827–28).
Hypothesis The indefinite article rendered as [e] rather than [ə] is a spelling pronunciation — NigE often resolves a reduced vowel to the full orthographic vowel, consistent with Gut's syllable-timing description and with Ihsane's framing of NigE as strongly orthography-driven (Ihsane, "Week 8: Nigerian English" (?)).
5 Conservative codas / spelling-driven /t/ Partially predictable
Evidence slabs [slæːbz] (retained), at [at] (fully released), thick [tɪk] — codas not simplified in this reading.
Rule Gut documents cluster simplification such as "hand → [han], post → [pɔs], cold → [kɔl]" (Gut 824); the passage contains no exact matches.
Hypothesis Reading-style carefulness elicited by the GMU passage suppresses the more extensive coda simplification Gut describes for conversational NigE, producing more conservative codas than would appear in spontaneous speech.

10. Indian English — Hindi1 (L1 Hindi — L2 proxy)

Speaker-substitution caveat. The L1 Hindi speaker is used as a proxy for IndE. IndE as described in Gargesh is itself heavily substrate-influenced by Hindi; the L2 speaker displays the same retroflex, stopping, and /v/~/w/ features. Read as an approximation.
[plis kal stela ask ɦ̆ɚ ʈu bɾɪŋ dız tɪŋks vɪt hɚ fɹʌm ðʌ sto sɪks spunz əv fɾeʃ sno piz faɪf θɪk slæbz ɔv blu tʃiz æn meɪbi ə snæk fɔɹ ə bɹʌðə(ɾ) bɒb vi also niɾə smal plastɪk sneɪk ændə bɪɡ tɔɪ fɹɑɡ fɚ ðə kɪdz ʃi kæn skub dɪz tɪŋz ɪntu tɹi ɾɛd bæɡz æn vi vɪl ɡo miʈ hə wɛnzdeɪ æt ʈə? tɾeɪn steɪʃ(ə̃)n]
1 Retroflex stops [ʈ, ɖ] for /t, d/ Predictable
Evidence to [ʈu], meet [miʈ], the [ʈə] (in at the train station).
Rule "T and D tend to be retroflexed as in the words certificate [sərʈifike:t] and London [lənɖən]" (Gargesh 997).
2 TH → dental/alveolar stops Predictable
Evidence thick [tɪk], things [tɪŋks]/[tɪŋz], three [tɹi], these [dɪz], the [ðʌ]~[də].
Rule "The dental fricatives /θ/ and /ð/ are non-existent in IndE. The aspirated voiceless stop [tʰ] is realized for /θ/; the voiced stop [d̪] is realized for /ð/" (Gargesh 998). The unaspirated [t] here is consistent with the separate claim that "voiceless stops are not aspirated in the syllable-initial position in IndE" (Gargesh 997).
3 V-for-W substitution Predictable
Evidence with [vɪt], we [vi], will [vɪl].
Rule "V often overlaps with W as in the realizations of the word power — [pa:vər] ~ [pa: wər]" (Gargesh 998).
4 Trilled / tapped rhotic with rhoticity Predictable
Evidence bring [bɾɪŋ], fresh [fɾeʃ], red [ɾɛd], need a [niɾə], brother [bɹʌðə(ɾ)], for [fɔɹ], her [hɚ].
Rule "The liquid /r/ is generally trilled . . . This is true of postvocalic /r/ as well" (Gargesh 998).
5 Final obstruent devoicing Partly unpredictable
Evidence five [faɪf] (devoiced /v/), scoop [skub] (ambiguous), of [ɔv] (retained).
Rule Gargesh's chapter describes consonantal features principally at the dental fricative, retroflex, and /v/~/w/ loci (Gargesh 998) and does not foreground final-obstruent devoicing.
Hypothesis Likely substrate L1-Hindi transfer rather than an institutionalised IndE feature — Hindi phonotactics reduce final voicing contrasts. Because the speaker is L1 Hindi (see caveat), the feature may reflect individual L2 interference rather than the stable IndE system Gargesh catalogues.

11. Singapore English — Mandarin12 (L1 Mandarin — L2 proxy)

Speaker-substitution caveat. The L1 Mandarin speaker is used as a proxy for CollSgE, whose substrate pressures come primarily from Hokkien/Mandarin/Malay (Wee 1017). The speaker displays the consonant-deletion, glottalisation, and devoicing features Wee catalogues. Read as an approximation; Mandarin-specific TH substitutions are flagged in the hypothesis for Feature 4.
[pʰliːz kʰɒl stelə əs hə tʰu bɾĩŋ diːz θĩŋz wɪθ hɚ fɹʌm də sto sɪks spʉnz əv fɹɛʃ snou pʰiːz faɪv tɪk slæbʰs əv blu tʃiːz æn meɪbi ə snækˀ əv hə bɹʌðə bɒb wi ɒlso nid ə smɒːl plastɪk sneɪ æ̃n ə bɪɡ tʰɔɪ fɹɒ fə ðə kɾdz ʃi kən skup dız ʃĩŋz ɪntu tʃi ɹɛd bæks æn wi wi gou miʔ hə wẽnzdei æt ðə tɾẽm steɪʃə̃n]
1 Final consonant deletion Predictable
Evidence snake [sneɪ] (no /k/), frog [fɹɒ] (no /g/), ask [əs] (no /k/).
Rule "stops are deleted in final position after continuant" — giving "limp [lɪm], cent [sɛn], stink [stɪŋ]" (Wee 1027); also "the stops may on occasion themselves get deleted so that the word then ends in a glottal stop" (Wee 1028).
2 Glottal-stop replacement for final /t/ Predictable
Evidence meet [miʔ], snack [snækˀ] (pre-glottalised); at [æt] (fully released — the rule is variable).
Rule Stops in final position "are often unreleased . . . the stops may on occasion themselves get deleted so that the word then ends in a glottal stop, as in like [laɪʔ] and hit [hɪʔ]" (Wee 1028).
3 Final obstruent devoicing Predictable
Evidence slabs [slæbʰs] (devoiced /z/), bags [bæks] (devoiced /g/ and /z/). Compare RP/GA [slæbz, bæɡz].
Rule "voiced obstruents commonly become devoiced when in word-final position" (Wee 1027).
4 Variable TH-realisation Partly predictable
Evidence Pre-vocalic [d] — these [dız], the [də], this [dɪz]. Alveolar [t] — thick [tɪk]. Preserved [θ] — things [θĩŋz]. Idiosyncratic — things [ʃĩŋz] (2nd); three [tʃi].
Rule "the interdental fricatives tend to be realized as [t, d] when pre-vocalic and [f] when at the end of a word" (Wee 1025).
Hypothesis [θ] retention, [tʃ] for three, and [ʃ] for things likely reflect substrate L1-Mandarin interference rather than the institutionalised CollSgE pattern — Mandarin lacks interdentals, so Mandarin L1 speakers can produce idiosyncratic replacements differing from Wee's CollSgE norms. Flagged because the speaker is L2 Mandarin (see caveat).
5 No KIT/FLEECE length contrast Predictable
Evidence Long — please [pʰliːz], peas [pʰiːz]; short — need [nid], scoop [skup], meet [miʔ]. Length is not systematic.
Rule "there is no length contrast so that any length difference tends to be sporadic . . . the distinction found in RP . . . in pairs like pool/pull or beat/bit is absent in CollSgE" (Wee 1025–26).
